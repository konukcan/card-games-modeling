## TL;DR

Night 3 ended with 12 of 18 rules where MCMC and enumeration diverged, primarily because **10–33 % of MCMC's posterior mass landed on programs the enumeration pool didn't contain**. The April-22 pre-flight (`RESIDUAL_FINDING.md`) ruled out probe instability (Source E) and depth-escape (Source C) and identified **budget-cap truncation** as the most likely remaining suspect.

**Night 4 ran the C2 test (depth-7 enum × 500k programs) to falsify or confirm budget-cap. Result: falsified.** Going from d6 / 300k to d7 / 500k yielded ~0 % rescue across all 6 sentinel rules (table below).

The residual is therefore **structural**, not budgetary: MCMC's proposal kernel reaches programs the enum top-down generator simply doesn't produce, regardless of depth or budget. Both sample from the same grammar specification, but they cover overlapping but non-identical subsets of the depth-bounded space.

Reading guide:
- **Δmap = d7_mapped − d6_mapped** → "rescue rate from C2 (richer enum)". Zero across the board.
- **d7_residual (Source A)** → mass MCMC visits that *neither* d6 nor d7 enum can reach. The structural floor.
- **B** = type-invalid programs (e.g., predicate type mismatch) — cannot be in any enum pool.
- D (pruner-rejected) is uniformly 0.000 and omitted.

For tight-agree rules the structural residual is < 1 %. For the divergent rules it's small in absolute terms (0.9 – 5.5 %), but for `four_of_a_kind_adjacent` the bigger story is the 31 % unmapped that's mostly Source C (depth > 6 programs) — see `synthesis/four_of_a_kind_adjacent_attribution_d7.json` for the per-program decomposition.

## Mechanism — why does MCMC reach programs enum doesn't?

Spot check on `all_red` (from `RESIDUAL_FINDING.md`):

| MCMC sample (not in enum pool)        | Structural peer that **is** in enum pool         | Difference |
|----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|------------|
| `(λ gt (max_rank $0) 3)`              | `(λ lt 5 (max_rank $0))`                          | flipped ≤/≥ |
| `(λ lt 0 (n_unique_ranks $0))`        | peers with same `n_unique_ranks` primitive       | different shape |
| `(λ gt (sum_ranks $0) 5)`             | `(λ gt (sum_ranks $0) 3)`                         | different constant |

Both elements of each pair are valid programs of the same grammar. Enum's top-down enumeration emits *one* canonical form per equivalence class (its syntactic-pruning collapses some isomorphic forms early). MCMC's subtree-replacement kernel happily re-derives the *other* canonical form via a different proposal path. The enum pool then doesn't contain the MCMC-visited form, even though it's present in the same equivalence class.

This is structural, not numeric. No amount of depth or budget makes enum re-emit forms it canonicalized away.

## What this changes for the broader project

1. **`mass_mapped` is the right validity gate**, but the floor it can hit is < 1 (rule-dependent). Before Night 4 we suspected the gap was a fixable enum truncation issue; after Night 4 we know it's an architectural property of the two samplers.

2. **Reporting plan**. Anywhere the project compares MCMC to enum-derived posteriors, flag mass_mapped explicitly and cite Night-4's structural-residual finding as the lower bound on perfect agreement.

3. **No re-enumeration trip wire**. Future "MCMC and enum disagree" results should not default to "run enum bigger" — Night 4 is the citation that says "no, that won't help."

## Open questions for next session

1. **Coverage gap formalism.** What fraction of valid, well-typed, depth-bounded programs in the grammar does enum's top-down generator miss, vs. what fraction can MCMC reach? Worth quantifying so we can either extend enum's enumeration order to cover the gap, or formally accept the structural residual as a lower bound.

2. **C1 (longer MCMC) data is undigested.** `mcmc_100k_4chains/` (2.7 MB) is the Night-4 MCMC re-run with 100k steps × `piecewise_half` β schedule (longer / cleaner anneal than Night 3's 20k linear). Its convergence diagnostics haven't been compared against Night 3's. This addresses Source A (MCMC under-convergence) and would clarify whether the 5–10 % residual on `all_even` etc. is structural floor or still mixing.

3. **Generalization beyond the 6 sentinel rules.** Night 4's synthesis tested 6 rules (3 tight-agree, 1 borderline, 2 divergent). Extending to the full 18-rule Night 3 set would let us check whether the "tight rules have < 1 % structural floor" pattern holds universally.

4. **Are there enum-only programs that MCMC misses?** Night 4 measured "MCMC ⊄ enum"; the symmetric question "enum ⊄ MCMC" wasn't computed. The original Night 3 design doc parked it as "Question B" pending Question A's resolution. Now that Question A is answered, Question B is unblocked.
